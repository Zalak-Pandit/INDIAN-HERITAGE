About Assam

Assam, located in the northeastern part of India, is known for its rich culture, biodiversity, and vibrant festivals. It is famous for its tea gardens, producing some of the world's finest tea. The state boasts lush landscapes, including the Brahmaputra River, Kaziranga National Park, and numerous wildlife sanctuaries, making it a haven for nature lovers and wildlife enthusiasts. Assam's cultural heritage is showcased through its traditional music, dance forms like Bihu, and the warm hospitality of its people. The state's diverse population contributes to a unique blend of traditions, languages, and cuisines, offering visitors a memorable experience.

Discover the most engaging places

Kaziranga National Park

A UNESCO World Heritage Site, Kaziranga is renowned for its population of the Indian one-horned rhinoceros. The park is also home to tigers, elephants, wild water buffalo, and a variety of bird species.Jeep safaris and elephant rides offer excellent opportunities for wildlife viewing.

Majuli

Majuli, the world's largest river island, is situated on the Brahmaputra River. It is a cultural hub, famous for its vibrant festivals, satras (Vaishnavite monasteries), and traditional Assamese arts and crafts.Exploring the satras, experiencing local culture, and enjoying scenic boat rides.

Kamakhya Temple

Located on Nilachal Hill in Guwahati, Kamakhya Temple is one of the most revered Shakti Peethas in India. It is an important pilgrimage site and is particularly famous for the annual Ambubachi Mela.

Taste the flavours of assam

Pani Hamuk

A unique dish featuring water snails cooked with traditional spices.

Khaar

A traditional Assamese dish made with raw papaya, pulses, taro, and meat or fish, cooked with a unique ingredient called "Khaar," derived from sun-dried banana peel.

Til Pitha

it's a traditional Assamese sweet snack made especially during the Bihu festivals, particularly Magh Bihu. It is a type of rice cake that is stuffed with a sweet filling made of sesame seeds (til) and jaggery (molasses).

Laksa

A popular noodle soup dish with a tangy and spicy broth made from fish, tamarind, and various aromatic spices.

Handicrafts from the villages


Assam Silk

Muga Silk: Exclusive to Assam, known for its natural golden sheen and durability. Pat Silk: Known for its brilliant white color and smooth texture. Eri Silk: Also called 'Ahimsa Silk', it is known for its warmth and softness.

Pith Craft

Toys and Dolls: Made from a soft, lightweight material called pith (Indian cork), these are intricately designed and painted.


Bell Metal and Brass Craft

Utensils: Traditional Assamese utensils like Sarai (trays), Ban Bati (bowls), and Kalah (water pots). Decorative Items: Figurines and religious items made from bell metal and brass.

Bihu Festival

The Bihu festival is the most significant and widely celebrated festival in Assam, symbolizing the agrarian culture and the changing seasons. There are three main types of Bihu festivals: Rongali or Bohag Bihu, Kongali or Kati Bihu, and Bhogali or Magh Bihu


Rongali Bihu (Bohag Bihu)

Celebrations:
Goru Bihu: The first day is dedicated to the care of livestock, where cows and bulls are bathed and adorned.
Manuh Bihu: The second day is for people, marked by wearing new clothes, seeking blessings from elders, and socializing.
Husori and Bihu Dance: Groups of young men and women perform traditional Bihu dances and songs, visiting homes to spread joy.
Feasting: Traditional Assamese dishes like pithas (rice cakes), larus (sweet balls), and other delicacies are prepared and shared.

Common Elements in Bihu Celebrations

Bihu Dance and Music: Integral to all three Bihu festivals, these performances involve energetic dance movements and folk songs, accompanied by traditional instruments like the dhol (drum), pepa (buffalo horn pipe), and gogona (mouth harp).
Community Involvement: Bihu is a community festival, with people of all ages participating in the festivities, fostering social harmony and cultural unity.
Traditional Attire: People wear traditional Assamese attire, such as the Mekhela Chador for women and Dhoti-Gamosa for men, during the celebrations.

Kongali Bihu (Kati Bihu)

Timing: Observed in mid-October, during the autumn season. Significance: Reflects a period of scarcity, as it falls during the lean period before the harvest.

Celebrations:
Lighting of Lamps: Earthen lamps (sakis) are lit in paddy fields, gardens, and near the Tulsi plant to ward off evil spirits and pests. Rituals: People perform rituals to protect the crops and pray for a good harvest. Tulsi Plant Worship: The Tulsi plant is worshipped, and small earthen lamps are placed around it.
