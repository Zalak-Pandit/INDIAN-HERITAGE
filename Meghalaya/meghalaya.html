About Meghalaya

Meghalaya, known as the "Abode of Clouds," is a picturesque state in northeastern India. Famous for its stunning landscapes, Meghalaya boasts lush green hills, enchanting waterfalls, and rich biodiversity. The state is home to the wettest place on earth, Mawsynram, and the living root bridges of Cherrapunji. Its capital, Shillong, often referred to as the "Scotland of the East," features charming colonial architecture and vibrant local culture. Meghalaya offers a serene retreat for nature lovers and adventure seekers, with activities ranging from trekking and caving to exploring pristine lakes .

Discover the most engaging places

Cherrapunji (Sohra):

Famous for its heavy rainfall, Cherrapunji features stunning landscapes, including the majestic Nohkalikai Falls and living root bridges.




Shillong:

The capital city, known as the "Scotland of the East," offers a mix of colonial charm and natural beauty, with attractions like Ward's Lake, Shillong Peak, and the Don Bosco Museum.

Mawsynram

The wettest place on earth, Mawsynram is known for its scenic beauty and unique rock formations, such as the Mawjymbuin Cave.

Handicrafts from the villages

Mask Making

Masks are crafted for use in traditional dance and ceremonies. They often feature elaborate designs and are made from wood or other materials.

Embroidery and Needlework

Embroidery techniques vary among tribes. For example, the Khasis are known for their mohair embroidery, while the Garos use colorful threads to create vibrant patterns on their traditional clothing.

Bamboo and Cane Craft

Skilled artisans create intricate designs using bamboo and cane, which are abundant in the region. Items often feature traditional patterns and are valued for their durability and aesthetic appeal.

The Wangala Festival

The Wangala Festival, also known as the "100 Drums Festival," is a prominent festival celebrated by the Garo tribe in Meghalaya.

History and Significance

Purpose:
The Wangala Festival is held to celebrate the harvest and to pay homage to Misi Saljong, the Sun God, for a bountiful harvest. It is also a time to celebrate the Garo tribe’s cultural heritage and traditions.

Timing:
The festival typically takes place in October or November, marking the end of the harvest season.
Feasting:
A significant part of the Wangala Festival is the communal feasting. Various traditional dishes, including rice, meat, and vegetables, are prepared and shared among the participants.

Celebrations:

Drumming and Dance:
The festival is renowned for its energetic drumming and dance performances. Traditional drums, known as “Mit,” are played by a large group of people, creating a rhythmic and vibrant atmosphere. Dancers perform traditional dances dressed in colorful attire and adorned with traditional jewelry. The dances are performed in a circle, and participants move to the beat of the drums.


Attire: Participants wear traditional Garo costumes, which include intricately woven dresses and headgear made from feathers, beads, and other materials.

Cultural Significance:

The Wangala Festival is a celebration of the Garo tribe's cultural identity and heritage. It provides a platform for the community to come together, celebrate their traditions, and preserve their unique customs. Overall, the Wangala Festival is a vibrant and joyous celebration that showcases the rich cultural traditions of the Garo tribe and offers a glimpse into their way of life.


Community Involvement:
The festival is a community affair, with people from all age groups participating. It fosters a sense of unity and strengthens communal bonds among the Garo people.
